Review framework

Conceptual axes for filtering

Data regime

Correspondence, missingness, and granularity describe how strongly the modalities align and how demanding the observed data setting is.

Modelling formalism

Prior knowledge, latent design, and architecture place methods between informed, structured, inspectable formulations and agnostic, unstructured, black-box ones.

Application level

Application level distinguishes broadly reusable general-purpose models from application-driven formulations targeting a specific biological question.

Figure 1. Conceptual axes for classifying multimodal integration methods in biology. Multimodal integration methods can be positioned along three complementary dimensions. The data regime can be progressively relaxed from its most controlled form, namely paired, fully observed, single-level measurements, toward increasingly demanding settings with stronger missingness, weaker cross-modal correspondence, or multi-level granularity. The modelling formalism can be tightened or loosened independently of the data: a formulation may move toward stronger prior knowledge, more structured latent representations, and more inspectable architectures, or remain agnostic, unstructured, and black-box. Finally, the application level distinguishes broadly reusable general-purpose models from application-driven formulations targeting a specific biological question. Together, these axes situate any integration method within a common framework and make explicit what assumptions a method requires and what it gives up.
